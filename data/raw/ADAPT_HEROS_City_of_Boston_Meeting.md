## Boston's Chinatown Community

Chinatown is a vibrant community in downtown Boston, home to many working class Asian, immigrant families. It has a rich network and history of social services and civic engagement; environmental activism; family-owned businesses; cultural activities, theater, arts, and fine cuisine [11].

Based on the 2020 Census, there are close to 5,500 residents in Chinatown, with approximately 60% identifying as Asian, 49% foreign-born, and 59% speaking another language at home [11].

*Source: Mural on Oak Street in Boston's Chinatown, Northeastern University Library, 1976-2008*
*Source: 千絲萬縷 Tied Together by A Thousand Threads, Shaina Lu*

---

## Environmental Justice Concerns

Boston's Chinatown faces significant environmental injustices. Compared to other neighborhoods in Boston and statewide, Chinatown has the hottest temperatures, high air pollution levels, and high flooding risks [12].

These injustices are driven by racist urban planning policies and practices, such as the:
- Construction of major highways (Interstates 93 and 90) cutting through Chinatown
- Placement of transportation terminals (train, bus, airline) and industrial activities in close proximity
- Lack of green spaces and parks
- High density of dark and impervious surfaces (i.e., asphalt, concrete)

In addition, as climate change progresses, Chinatown will experience even hotter temperatures, higher air pollution, and increased risk of flooding and other extreme events [12].

*Source: City of Boston, Heat Resilience Solutions for Boston Final Report, April 2022*

## What Community Residents Want

Chinatown residents have long advocated for well-designed, climate-resilient, accessible, and inclusive open spaces that promote active use for children and residents of all ages.

In 2019, the Asian Community Development Corporation (ACDC) and several community-based organizations launched the Open Space Strategy for Chinatown. This initiative engaged ~250 community members and artists in a series of community visioning workshops and interviews with 9 local businesses.

Participants reported dissatisfaction with the "quality of open space in Chinatown" and "wish to see more open spaces that promote active use" such as parks, playgrounds, community gardens, recreational space, and outdoor fitness [16].

## History of Environmental Activism

### 1950s–1970s
Hundreds of families in Boston's Chinatown were displaced by highway construction and development projects, including the expansion of Tufts-New England Medical Center (TNEMC), the Central Artery, and the Massachusetts Turnpike. Although unable to prevent highway construction, the On Leong Merchants Association and Garment Industry fought to minimize the damage and came to a compromise to keep part of the Merchants Association building [17]. The South End neighborhood was also impacted by Urban Renewal. To combat this, the Chinese community began guerilla gardening in spaces where buildings were torn down, which became what is now known as Berkeley Community Gardens [18].

### 1960s
Urban renewal projects, including the West End project and TNCMC expansion further threatened the Boston Chinatown community and housing. John Collins was elected mayor due to outrage regarding West End urban renewal and the Central Artery. Collins reorganized the Boston Redevelopment Authority, minimizing land takings, improving compensation for displaced residents, and requiring public participation for planning. The Chinese Consolidated Benevolent Association further worked with community residents and advocated for their interests. They created a plan that set boundaries for TNEMC's expansion, reorganized street patterns, and identified sites for affordable housing, community facilities, and a new Quincy School [17].

### 1970s–1980s
The South Cove Urban Renewal Plan allowed for the development of the Mass Pike Towers and Tai Tung Village (owned by the Chinese Consolidated Benevolent Association), Quincy Towers and other smaller projects, which created about 2000 units of subsidized housing. However, there was not enough housing to meet the needs of the growing population, which led to many families to leave Chinatown for less dense neighborhoods, such as Brighton, Brookline, and Newton [17].

This period further gave rise to a new group of activists and organizations, including the Chinese Economic Development Corporation, Chinese American Civic Association, The Golden Age Center, South Cove Community Health Center, and the new Quincy Community School [17].

### 1991
The South End Lower Roxbury Open Space Land Trust (SELROSLT) was created to preserve community gardens and open spaces in the South End and officially created the Berkeley Street Community Gardens [18].

### Early 2000s
SELROSLT began a capital improvement plan to further improve Berkeley Community Gardens as it continues to grow and change [18].

### 2010 and 2020
Community members collaborated on the development of the 2010 and 2020 Master Plans. Both Plans stressed the importance for affordable housing, a cultural center, library, open space, education and social services [17].

## Study Design

Our study collected air pollution, heat stress, and other environmental data in July and August of 2023 using two low-cost sensors installed at 12 open spaces around Chinatown (see Figure 1). The two sensors were: the Kestrel (Kestrel DROP D3FW Fire Weather Monitor), which collected data on wet bulb globe temperature (WBGT) and ambient temperature, and the Purple Air (PurpleAIR PA-II-FLEX), which collected data on fine particulate matter (PM2.5), a major air pollutant of concern. These sensors collected data continuously at the 10-minute interval, and for this report, we averaged the data to hourly.

The data were reviewed and cleaned (e.g., we removed data points that may be due to poor sensor performance). We also applied correction factors to the PM2.5 data based on methods developed by the U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA).

We also compared air pollution data at each site with data collected publicly from the MassDEP Federal Equivalent Method (FEM) monitor. The FEM monitor is located at the corner of Lincoln and Kneeland Streets next to the 93 South highway ramp (please see the blue diamond in Figure 1).

To complement the sensor data, we conducted daily site visits over a two-week period in July 2023. During these visits, we recorded observations about traffic levels, surface temperatures, site features, pavement types, trees and vegetation types, and activity at each site, among other relevant environmental factors.

> **Note:** We did not have access to temperature data from the MassDEP FEM monitor for site-specific comparisons.

> **Note:** Eliot Norton WBGT data was unavailable for July weeks 27 to week 29 due to Kestrel Sensor malfunction. Data were collected by the Kestrel sensors in 10-minute intervals between July 5–August 23, 2023. National Weather Service (NWS) Guidelines: ≥ 80°F will begin to stress the body.

## HEAT: Estimated WBGT by Site and Day of Week

Temperatures varied across sites by 0.30 to 1.8°F, after accounting for weather patterns, time, and fine particulate matter.

*Model adjusted for: time of day, day of week, month, wind speed, wind direction, relative humidity, and fine particulate matter.*

- **Castle Square & Oxford Place** had highest estimated temperatures.
- **Berkeley Community Gardens** had lowest estimated temperatures.
- Temperatures fluctuated by days of the week, with highest levels on Thursdays and Fridays.

## AIR POLLUTION: PM2.5 in July and August 2023

PM2.5 were higher in July than August, reaching up to 60 µg/m³. Extreme levels varied across sites.

Outliers across all open space sites represent consistently high PM2.5 levels, possibly due to wildfires in Canada in July (07/17–07/19/2023).

| Month | Median PM2.5 | Maximum PM2.5 |
|---|---|---|
| **July 七月** | 9.8 µg/m³ | 46.2 µg/m³ |
| **August 八月** | 7.3 µg/m³ | 42.6 µg/m³ |

- **Data period:** July 5–31, 2023 (July); August 1–23, 2023 (August)

> **Note:** Data were collected by the Purple Air monitors in 10-minute intervals between July 5–August 23, 2023. WHO guidelines: within a 24-hour period, PM2.5 exposure should not exceed 15 μg/m³ for more than 3–4 days per year.

## AIR POLLUTION: Estimated PM2.5 by Site and Day of Week

PM2.5 varied across some sites by 0.01 to 0.15 µg/m³, after accounting for weather patterns, time, and ambient temperature.

## HEROS Summary & Recommendations

### Reduce Heat
- Ambient temperatures slightly varied across open spaces
- Surface temperature widely fluctuated for pavements and surfaces (e.g. bench, playground equipment)
- **Recommendations:**
  - Cool surfaces (pavements, play structures)
  - Increase tree/vegetation density and diversity
  - Increase shade structures and mechanical cooling (i.e. misters)
  - Permanent cooling center in Chinatown

### Reduce Air Pollution
- Varied across open space sites, particularly near major roads
- **Recommendations:**
  - Increase tree/vegetation and roadside barriers
  - Indoor spaces for recreation and physical activity
  - Reduce traffic corridors and parking

### Sites for Improvements
- **City of Boston:** Lyndeboro Gardens, Phillips Square, Tai Tung, Elliot Norton Park
- **Privately-owned:** Oxford Place, Castle Square, One Greenway
- **MassDOT:** Reggie Wong Park
